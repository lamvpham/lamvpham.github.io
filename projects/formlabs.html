PERMISSIONS ARCHITECTURE

Defining the entity relationships for user permissions in a 3D printer ecosystem

Context

The Problem

With a new feature that enables workflows to be scaled up, the base Dashboard interface was not compatible with this scaling and only added confusion for users.

How might we improve user's mental models of the Dashboard UX architecture for better user permissions & workflows?

Role

I spent 10 weeks defining the user/team/group permissions architecture (shortforming it to PA) in the product, Dashboard. The responsibilities of my role included working with the Dashboard product manager and designer to understand and architect a more usable platform due to a new addition of enterprise scaling (Fleet Control).

Solution

My final prototypes have a new PA for Dashboard based on market landscape analysis, architecture iterations, and high-fidelity sign up, onboarding, and company setting flows. This design affected 4.2% of customer accounts (~50 companies), helping simplify their mental models and user flows.

Introduction

Understanding the Problem Space

With 10 weeks to research products with PA's similar & different from Dashboard and then extrapolate the insights into an PA suitable for the platform, I had to first understand the user pain points. With the current UX architecture, what pain points arise from usage? What user feedback was given about the platform?

Based on user's current issues with the platform, my PM and I defined these goals to guide our direction for the architecture.

Permission Architecture Iteration

Based on the analysis, my PM and I conceptualized 17 different user permission architectures. Each have their own pros and cons, alongside their respective market landscape comparisons - so we scorecard each based on the goals defined.

After analyzing the different PA options and their trade-offs, we chose this architecture as it aligned best with our goals compared to the current version of Teams and Groups.

MVP

Validation

Myself, my Dashboard PM, and the Dashboard designer got together with the CPO to discuss the architecture. This option only requires two levels of hierarchy, was easiest for (new & current) users to understand, and encapsulates all teams to be in one organization, so after, it was time to start prototyping.

Results

After some lo-fi sketches to map out the designs, they were converted into hi-fi prototypes of the Sign Up/Onboarding screens and the Company Settings screens.

Moving Forward

Next Steps

With the handoff of the designs to the Dashboard designer, the next step is to further refine the details of the workflows, alongside mapping out the financial pricing plan for enterprise scaling due to the architectural changes.

What I Learned

The project impacted ~50 companies with >100 employees and >4 printers each. This revamp was internally tested and showcased over 80% increase in user flow comprehension.

Throughout this project, it taught me higher-level product decisions and how to communicate UX copy more effectively. Moving forward from this internship, I want to keep tackling complex problems that deal with access and user permissions to further refine my skills with role-based designs.
